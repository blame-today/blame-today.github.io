why this matters

a silly idea that grew architecture
blame.today started as a dumb thought i'd had for years: a place to publicly blame anyone or anything, one vote per device per day, that's it. no logins. no profiles. just a daily blame board.
the architecture wasn't the goal. it emerged. once i tried to actually build it for free, free-tier composability became a constraint. once i looked at what it would display (real people, real institutions, real grievances), transparency became the next constraint. what fell out of those two was a pattern that turned out to be more interesting than the app.
so this page is the writeup of an accident. the silly idea is in the middle; the serious-sounding architecture wrapped itself around the silly idea, on inspection. if any of it's useful to you, that's a bonus. this isn't a company, it's a proof-of-concept i did for fun.

mission (since apparently i need one)

ok — at this point i probably need a mission statement or something. i say "we" all over this page but there's just me. =P so:

blame.today is a fun free app for tapping who you blame today. the daily anonymous public record of those taps is, on inspection, an interesting real-time read on the negative-sentiment zeitgeist. the architecture i used to ship it for $4.19 turns out to be a reusable pattern.

each of those is bonus to the one above it. if only the first works, great — i shipped a fun thing. if the second works too, i've made something with a small public utility. if all three work, the pattern does work elsewhere. i'm trying to make all three possible. i'm not trying to control which one matters.

realistically though: i'll ship it to the app store, the votes will mostly come from my testing, i'll show some friends, and that'll be the end. lol. that's the actual base case. but building it has been fun, it raised a bunch of interesting questions on the way, and the writeup of those questions is here on the off chance someone wants to read them. that's enough. even if the app dies quietly, the page persists.

what's the goal?

the goal has three layers, and they showed up in this order:

1 · the silly surface

a daily blame board. who do you blame today? tap, done. you ranked. one vote per device per day. no profiles, no logins, no algorithm, no audience. it's funny. that's it. that's the whole pitch on the home page.

2 · capturing the negative-sentiment zeitgeist

this part showed up while i was figuring out how to seed the data. a permanent, anonymous, daily public record of what's pissing people off — across politics, weather, work, traffic, brands, technology, abstractions, family, the algorithm itself. nothing else does this. social media is full of negativity but it's named, performative, and monetized for engagement. blame.today is one tap, anonymous, permanent, free. just the sentiment. no profile to maintain, no thread to defend, no audience to play to.

3 · prove the pattern is buildable

this part i only really saw in retrospect, after the architecture had emerged. the architecture isn't the goal — but it's the test. if a one-person proof-of-concept can ship the property "you can't trust the company running it, but you can verify the math in ten seconds," anyone who needs that property for something more serious knows it's possible. the real artifact isn't the app or the architecture. it's the existence proof.

what this isn't

before any of the "look at this architecture" stuff, calibration. just to be clear about what i'm not claiming:

  • not an authority on anything. the leaderboard is just "the aggregate of people who happen to use blame.today today." it's not representative of the public, not a moral verdict, not a measure of who deserves blame. it's who got tapped.
  • not journalism. seeded counts come from news headlines via an LLM. they're a vibe check, not reporting. if you want to know what's actually happening, read the news directly — the headlines that drove an entity into the seeds are visible in the iOS app's tap-row view, so you can verify what fed the model.
  • not accountability tech. the pattern is reusable for serious accountability use cases. this app isn't one of those. blame.today is the demo, not the application.
  • not trying to influence anything. no feed. no algorithm. no trending notifications. no engagement metric. you tap, you see the board, you close the app. that's it.
  • not a company. one person, a weekend, $4.19, and a domain. if it gets enough traction to need to become something more, that's a different problem to solve later. right now: fun app, free, no business model, no ambitions for one.

ultimately this is just a fun free app for doing a kind of fun and silly thing. it surfaced all this other stuff on inspection, but that's not what it's for. if any of the above changes, you'll see it on the public ledger. if it doesn't, well — i'll keep paying the $4.19 once a year. =P

stress-testing the property

the interesting test of an architecture isn't how it behaves when everything works. it's how gracefully it fails — or how it'd hold up if the people running it changed their minds. a few scenarios:

what if you DDoS our cloudflare worker?

cloudflare's anycast edge absorbs L3/L4 floods automatically — that's free, planetary-scale, and applies to every plan including ours. the real risk is exhausting our 100k req/day worker quota. defense in layers:

  • edge caching on /leaderboard?day=X means 99% of reads never invoke the worker
  • rate-limit rule on the worker endpoints — abusive IPs get bounced at the edge, not against our quota
  • bot fight mode trims obvious scripts
  • param validation rejects cache-busting query strings before they hit the database

worst case if all of that fails: the worker returns 429 until UTC midnight, the iOS app shows the last-cached board (it's local-first), and the nostr relays keep accepting real votes regardless. the ledger doesn't depend on us.

with a $5/mo hard cap on the worker, the worst-case financial outcome is $5 burned. an attacker rents a botnet costing more than that. the cost of attacking us is bounded; their incentive is too.

what if cloudflare goes down?

the same cache logic runs on AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, GCP Cloud Run, Deno Deploy. clients try the next provider. the relays kept running through the entire CF outage. eventual consistency does the rest.

today we're only on cloudflare. multi-provider rollout is in the release plan — but the protocol is platform-agnostic, so switching deployment targets is a worker rewrite, not a data migration.

what if you start selling blames?

not the plan. blame.today is a fun PoC, not a business model. but sure, anything could happen — i'm one person, and one person could change their mind. if paid blames ever shipped, you'd see them on the public ledger. paid blames are still nostr events. the architecture doesn't have a private side-channel.

could i technically run a worse version of this with hidden votes? sure. that would also be a different app — and i'd be telling on myself the moment i quietly stopped publishing the corresponding events to /verify. so mostly i'd just be shipping my own scandal. that's the property doing its job: you couldn't start selling votes without people knowing. that's something, right? =P

what if we quietly miscount?

we publish the filter, the relay list, and the rules. anyone with a websocket can recompute today's leaderboard themselves. if our numbers diverge from the relays', we're lying — and it's detectable in seconds, not months. we cannot lie undetectably. that's the entire point.

show your work

the pitch is "you don't have to trust me." that means i owe the actual mechanisms, not just the architecture diagrams. three places people ask about most: how do you tell devices apart, what literally ends up on the public ledger, and how do you turn today's news into seed numbers. here's all three — with the abuse vectors i can think of and what i actually think about each.

your "device id" — what it really is

when the iOS app first launches, it generates a per-device nostr keypair locally (BIP-340 schnorr, stored in the system keychain). your public key is the device id we track. the worker only stores SHA-256(public-key) for the one-vote-per-device-per-day uniqueness check — not the key itself, not your name, not your Apple ID, not your IP.

on this marketing site, "you →" in the footer is a similar random identifier that lives in your browser's localStorage. it's nostr-pubkey-shaped (32 hex bytes) but it's not yet a real signing keypair — no signing happens from the web today. it's a slot.

it's not a fingerprint. we don't derive it from your device's characteristics (canvas hash, GPU, font list, audio stack, screen geometry). it's a random keypair generated locally on first launch. if you want a new id, delete the app and reinstall (with iCloud Keychain disabled), or clear your browser's localStorage for blame.today. you control it. we don't have a way to identify you across resets.

abuse vectors i can think of:

  • reinstall to vote again. partially mitigated by iCloud Keychain (your keypair survives reinstall if signed into iCloud). without that, yes, reinstall = new identity. is that realistic for a daily blame board? probably not. is it visible? yes — the pattern "this device just appeared, voted for X, never seen before" is on the public ledger.
  • own multiple devices, vote multiple times. yes. one person with N iPhones gets N votes. this is called "one vote per device per day," not "one vote per person per day." i'm not interested in collecting biometrics or government ID to enforce the latter.
  • sim farm / phone farm. someone with hundreds of devices could amplify a target. yields hundreds of pubkeys voting in lockstep — extremely visible on the ledger, costs the attacker real money, fails any smell test on inspection.
  • iCloud account switching. theoretically gives a new keychain entry. practical effort high, payoff low.

my opinion: "one vote per device per day" is a good-enough constraint to make the board interesting without imposing the surveillance cost of "one vote per person ever." attackers who try to game it are visible on the public ledger — auditors can detect unnatural patterns and call them out. i don't try to prevent abuse cryptographically; i surface it socially. that's the trade.

there's also a hedge most people miss: the seeded counts from the news zeitgeist (next card) act as a baseline of "what's actually being talked about right now." if 1000 sock-puppet votes pile onto some obscure target that's nowhere in today's news, the discrepancy is visible by contrast. the seeding isn't just for the empty-leaderboard problem — it's also a real-time sanity check on the user-vote distribution.

what actually goes on the public ledger

every vote becomes a nostr event with this content, literally: {"target":"<canonical-id>","label":"<display-label>","app":"blame","v":1} — plus the signer's pubkey, the timestamp, the signature, and a "d" tag identifying the day. that's it. seven relays we don't own each get a copy. it's plaintext, it's permanent, and we can't unpublish from a public protocol.

which means: if you type private info into the target field and it slips past our filters, that private info goes on the public ledger in plaintext. no encryption, no hashing.

why not just hash the target? because the leaderboard depends on grouping ("how many people blamed 'mondays'?"), which needs the plaintext to do the count. if we hash, we'd have to keep the plaintext mapping ourselves — which makes us the trusted authority and breaks the cache-vs-record property. auditors recomputing from raw events need to see plaintext too, or they can't recompute. the architecture requires plaintext on the chain. the only viable defense is at the input boundary, before the vote is ever signed — which is a substantial topic of its own, treated separately in moderation, in retrospect ↓.

seeding — how today's news becomes numbers

the daily board doesn't start empty. each morning at 6am UTC the worker pulls headlines from a fixed set of public sources, asks a small LLM to extract a blameable entity from each, and computes a seeded_count that estimates how much aggregate negative attention each entity is getting. seeded counts are clearly labeled separately from real_count in the API — they're never conflated with actual user votes.

the sources, with their attention weights per mention:

  • yahoo news RSS — 200,000 (high; direct mainstream publisher)
  • google news RSS — 50,000 (aggregator; many publishers deduped)
  • reddit /r/news, /r/worldnews — 30,000 each
  • reddit /r/politics — 25,000
  • reddit /r/technology — 20,000
  • wikipedia top pageviews (yesterday, en) — weight = actual pageview count, sqrt-compressed above 1M

the model and the formula:

  • LLM: @cf/meta/llama-3.1-8b-instruct on cloudflare Workers AI
  • per headline → { target, label, negativity } with negativity ∈ [0, 3]
  • aggregate per canonical id: attention = Σ source_weight, avg_negativity = Σ neg / mentions
  • seeded_count = round(0.001 × effective_attention × avg_negativity), where effective_attention is sqrt-compressed above 1M to keep one outlier from dominating
  • daily decay: existing trending × 0.85 each refresh, today's contributions added on top, anything below 5 pruned. ~30-day fade for a one-time mention.
  • leaderboard ranking uses bayesian shrinkage so each seeded vote counts as ≈ 1/10 of a real vote, fading further as real votes pile up

where this is heading: a council of models.

running everything through one llm has two problems beyond bias: every model is one company's opinion of "blameable entity," and any one free tier runs out of tokens fast. cloudflare workers ai is ~10k neurons/day; we currently use ~7k just for the daily refresh. that leaves no room for retries, no buffer for richer prompts, no headroom to scale headlines.

the plan is to fan the same headlines out to several small models in parallel — gemini-flash, llama-3.1-8b, mistral-small, qwen-2.5-7b, deepseek-chat — and take consensus on each entity. divergent picks get dropped or flagged as low-confidence. that does three things at once: hedges any single model's bias, produces safer cleaner word clouds when models agree, and spreads the load across multiple providers' free tiers. same "stack free tiers" pattern as the rest of the architecture, applied to inference.

llm-aggregator services make this nearly trivial to wire up — openrouter is the obvious one (one api, 100+ models, fan-out is a config change), with together, groq, anyscale all offering similar multi-model routing. some have free tiers; even paid is $0.10–$0.50 per million tokens for small models, which is rounding error in the running-costs ledger. the architectural property doesn't change — cache is opinion, protocol is record — but the cache gets smarter and more durable.

abuse vectors i can think of:

  • i could manipulate seeding to bias the board. technically yes. defense: the whole pipeline is deterministic from public inputs. you can run the same RSS feeds, the same model(s), the same code, and get the same numbers. when the main repo goes public near launch, the code is auditable. weight / source / model / formula changes are git-visible.
  • any single LLM has biases. yes, every model does. defense today: model id is public, you can run the same headlines through a different model and compare. defense in progress: the council pattern above turns this into a strength — divergence across models is the bias signal itself.
  • the news sources have biases. obviously. the weight calibration is open. you can disagree with the weights — they're literally numbers in a config. recompute with your weights and see how the board shifts.
  • i could quietly swap the formula. sure, but the deployed worker code is reproducible from the repo, and changes show up in git history. if the displayed seeded counts diverge from what the formula in the repo produces on the same inputs, something is wrong and it's detectable.

my opinion: seeded counts are an editorial choice presented as an editorial choice. they're labeled separately so a real vote count of 1 isn't conflated with a seeded count of 1,000. the iOS app's tap-row context view shows the actual headlines that drove an entity into the seeds — nothing is laundered. the seeds exist because an empty leaderboard is boring and because the zeitgeist baseline catches sock-puppet spam, not because i want to nudge anyone's votes.

moderation, in retrospect

this section is about a problem that wasn't on my radar at the start. for any reader who thought about it earlier than i did: yeah, you saw this coming. i didn't, for a while. then i did. here's the arc — partly so i don't get to pretend it was always part of the plan.

the original vision: zero moderation

initially i was going to ship it open. one vote per device per day, anonymous, signed, public. who's it hurting? this is, in retrospect, the kind of sentence you only get to say before you actually try to launch the thing.

if every user was using it as intended — one blame per day on an abstraction or a public figure, no PII — there'd be no problem. the worst-case input ("john smith at 123 main street") would land in millionth place behind war, ai, the algorithm, mondays and the daily news villains. it'd never surface. the leaderboard would be exactly the data the app is supposed to surface.

but reality has a small fraction of users who'll type their ex's address, paste a phone number, or attempt to dox someone for fun. once that string is signed and published to seven nostr relays we don't run, it's there forever. we can't unpublish from a public protocol. that's the entire point of the architecture, and it cuts both ways.

two things had to change

once i thought about actually shipping, two parts of the original plan had to evolve.

1. seeding became more important than i thought.

originally the news-seeding was just "fill an empty leaderboard so the first user has something to tap." in retrospect it's doing real structural work: the news signal is louder than any single user-typed target. a name nowhere in today's news and only voted on by a few users sinks to the bottom; a name everywhere in the news rises. seeded counts aren't decoration — they're the structural defense against vote-clustering on private targets.

this is also why i'm planning to extend the seed sources. today: news + wikipedia pageviews. next on the list: kalshi, polymarket and other prediction-market trending feeds — topics people are betting real money on are almost definitionally the hottest topics. then maybe reddit upvote velocity on a curated set of subs. each additional public source makes the zeitgeist signal louder relative to any single user-typed name.

to put a sharper edge on this: with five aggregated public sources feeding the seed signal (news, wikipedia, kalshi, polymarket, reddit-velocity), the top ~100 entries on any day are essentially locked in by the public zeitgeist. a coordinated bot army would have to overwhelm five aggregated public signals to push one PII entry into visible territory. that's a structurally very high bar — and the structure is defending the user, not the company. seed-source diversification isn't just a bias hedge; it's a moderation force multiplier.

2. moderation had to become automatic.

i'm one person. i can't review every vote, and i can't be awake every hour. so moderation has to:

  • run in the worker before the vote is signed and published — once it's on the relays, no take-backs
  • be cheap enough to not burn the daily inference budget
  • be deterministic enough to be reproducible from the same code — same input, same decision
  • fail closed under uncertainty. when in doubt, reject. the cost of a false positive (we rejected a legit vote) is recoverable — the user retries. the cost of a false negative (we let PII through) is forever.

what's running now, what's coming before launch

today, at the input layer:

  • length + word limits. targets are short. most accidental PII can't physically fit in the field.
  • regex blocks for phone numbers (\d{3}[-.\s]?\d{3}[-.\s]?\d{4}), emails (anything with @ and a TLD), SSN patterns (\d{3}-\d{2}-\d{4}), URL shapes. vote rejected before signing.
  • autocomplete bias. the trending list (news + wikipedia seeds) is the path of least resistance — most users tap a suggestion and never type a freeform target.

before app store submission:

  • LLM PII check at the worker, using the same council pattern as seeding. multiple small models, vote rejected if any council member flags PII or the consensus is uncertain. fail-closed by design.
  • public-figure heuristic. targets that match a wikipedia page or appear in today's news are explicitly allowed. unknown "first-last" name shapes that match neither get rejected as potentially private. errs toward "blame abstractions and public figures, not unknown individuals."

where the moderation lives (and doesn't)

important honest note: moderation lives at the acceptance boundary, before signing. it does NOT live at the retrieval boundary, after publication. a user running their own nostr client can technically publish any string they want to the relays — they don't need our worker. our worker is the cache; the relays are the record; the input filter is at the cache.

what that means for the leaderboard: our leaderboard reflects votes that passed our moderation. auditors recomputing from the raw relay feed will see everything published — including stuff our filter rejected if anyone bypassed our worker. that's a feature, not a bug: independent observers can see what we filtered out and disagree with us about it. our filter is auditable in both directions.

my opinion: i underestimated this at the start. "zero moderation, fully open" is a sentence you can say before you actually try to ship the thing. once you're staring at a real input box that a real user can type a real address into, the responsibility lands. the architecture is right — cache is opinion, protocol is record — but it puts the entire moderation burden on the input layer, because once a string is on the relays we can't pull it back. so the input layer has to be robust before any traffic of any size arrives. treating this as the real problem it is (instead of an edge case) is one of the main reasons launch isn't tomorrow.

if you find a way to slip PII past the filters: tell me. i'd rather hear it from a friendly tester than discover it after someone's home address is on seven relays for the next decade.

the flow, in retrospect

the user flow has the same shape as the architecture — i didn't design it on purpose, it found its current shape as i made decisions about what should be meaningful. three turns:

1 · originally: infinite-click ledger

first version was an open ledger. tap whatever, tap as often as you want, no limit, no friction. just contribute to a stream. sounds reasonable, until you think for two seconds: if every vote costs nothing, every vote means nothing. the leaderboard becomes "whoever has the most patience for tapping." that's not a signal, that's an endurance test.

2 · then: one vote per device per day

next version: one tap. one device. one day. that's it. now your vote is scarce — you only get one, you have to spend it on something, and once you do you're locked out until tomorrow. scarcity does the work that infinite-tap couldn't: scarcity makes a vote weigh something.

as a side effect, this also fixed the "endurance leaderboard" problem. the board now reflects what a representative slice of device-holders chose to spend their one daily vote on. that's a meaningfully different signal.

3 · then: gate the board behind voting

latest turn: you have to vote (or actively choose not to) to see the full ranking. on the home page, the leaderboard is hidden behind a tap. tap the gate, the board reveals.

this is a deliberate call-to-action. blame.today is participatory. if you scroll, scroll, scroll without engaging, the page is telling you something — there's a threshold to cross. you can cross it casually (the gate tap isn't a real vote, just a confirmation that you're here on purpose) or you can skip it entirely (the data's on the public ledger, here's how to recompute). both paths are explicit.

important honest note: even with no users, this was the idea. a call-to-action with no traffic to act on it is still a call-to-action — it just isn't seen by anyone yet. the gate is designed for the day people show up, and it's fine if that day never comes. the framing matters whether the audience does or doesn't.

+ the sampler: a teaser before the gate

the home page also shows three random board entries above the gate — picked deterministically from your visitor key + today's date. same picks for you on every visit today; different picks for everyone else; new picks tomorrow.

this isn't the ranking. it's a taste. the goal is to convey "this is alive and there's something for you here" without revealing the rank — and to use the visitor key meaningfully, not just decoratively. you have an identity at blame.today the moment you arrive; the sampler is the first thing that identity does for you.

seed is SHA256-shaped(visitor_key + ':' + today); pool is top 100 entries; pick is the first 3 of the seeded shuffle. all client-side javascript — the worker just returns the leaderboard, the deterministic personalization happens in your browser. no per-visitor tracking on the server side. anyone can recompute their own sample given just their visitor key + the day.

where else this pattern fits

the cargo we're carrying is: cache is opinion, protocol is record. anywhere that property would matter:

  • public votes that need to stay verifiable — town meetings, HOA decisions, parent councils, community moderation
  • reputation systems that shouldn't be locked to one company — contractor reviews, restaurant trust, content authenticity
  • disclosure registries — lobbying contacts, public-spending dashboards, election counts, regulatory filings
  • accountability tools in general — anywhere "the org publishing the data" has incentive to bend the numbers, removing that ability is the point

each one is "blame.today but for X." pick the X. the protocol is the same; the surface changes. if blame works, those are the next vehicles.

the order it actually happened

i didn't sit down to design "a transparent public protocol for accountability data." i sat down to make a daily blame board because the idea kept making me laugh. then i needed it to be free. then i needed it to be honest. then i looked at what i'd built and noticed the pattern would work for any application where the publishing org has incentive to bend the numbers.

if you were designing this on purpose, you'd start with the architecture and pick a serious application. but designing it on purpose costs three years of regulatory review and a legal team. designing it by accident — because you wanted a daily blame board — costs a weekend.

so the order matters. the architecture is what it is because the surface was low-stakes. it never had to clear an approval bar; it just had to be cheap and honest, in that order. silly project, serious pattern, in that order. not the other way around.

why now

the idea has been bouncing around my head for roughly twenty years. i didn't have time, the tools, or the cheap infrastructure. now i have all three — AI pair-programming, free-tier composability, and a weekend. none of those existed in serviceable form when the idea first occurred to me. when they did line up, the cost of trying collapsed.

there's a thing i tell people i work with about estimates: the analysis is sometimes 80% of the work. by the time you understand a problem well enough to know what the right step is, you're already most of the way through that step. committing to a serious analysis of a problem is usually committing to producing the solution — or else you're just throwing money at something and not really understanding how far along you'll be at the end of the analysis.

blame.today is that. to genuinely understand whether this kind of free, transparent, permanent-by-protocol app was buildable, i had to get close enough to actually build it. once you're 80% there for free, you might as well ship the last 20%. so here we are.

tl;dr

blame.today is a silly idea that accidentally grew an architecture. it's a daily blame board where you can't trust the person running it to count honestly — and it doesn't matter, because you can recompute the numbers yourself in ten seconds. the whole thing costs $4.19 to operate. that wasn't the plan; that's what fell out of trying to build a free anonymous voting app and then realizing transparency mattered once i saw what it'd display.
